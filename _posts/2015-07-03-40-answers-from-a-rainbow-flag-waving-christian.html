Pastor Kevin DeYoung posted 40 Questions for Christians Now Waving Rainbow Flags. Here is my response:

  1. How long have you believed that gay marriage is something to be celebrated?

    I have supported same-sex marriage at least since I came to terms with my sexuality, but I never opposed same-sex marriage.

    How long have you believed that same-sex marriage is something to be condemned?

  2. What Bible verses led you to change your mind?

    As I was coming to terms with the fact that I cannot change my sexuality, I studied Scripture and learned that all direct references to homosexual behavior are in the context of shrine temple sex or idolatry. I learned that what I had be taught about homosexuality was wrong.

  3. How would you make a positive case from Scripture that sexual activity between two persons of the same sex is a blessing to be celebrated?

    There is no condemnation of loving, committed same-sex relations in the Bible. I believe that God’s declaration in Genesis 2:18 applies in full force to people of all sexual orientations. It is "not good" that we be alone, and when we find the suitable partner that God has for us, we simply want the same type of relationship that opposite-sex couples enjoy.

    How would you make a positive case from Scripture that sexual activity between two persons of the same sex is always condemned?

  4. What verses would you use to show that a marriage between two persons of the same sex can adequately depict Christ and the church?

    I see no verses that state that two persons of the same sex cannot adequately depict Christ and the church. Scripture’s depiction of David and Jonathan’s love is uses the same language that is used to refer to the love embodied by opposite-sex couples. After Jonathan’s death ended their 15-or-so year relationship, Scripture tells us that David married Princess Michal because he wanted to become Saul’s son-in-law. The text never says that David loved Princess Michal. Scripture is clear that the the souls of David and Jonathan were knit together and that David loved Jonathan "more than any woman." To me, the story of David and Jonathan does not read like a mere friendship.

    What verses would you use to show that marriage between two persons of the same sex cannot adequately depict Christ and the church?

  5. Do you think Jesus would have been okay with homosexual behavior between consenting adults in a committed relationship?

    Yes.

  6. If so, why did he reassert the Genesis definition of marriage as being one man and one woman?

    He didn't. In Matthew 19, Jesus was answering a questio about divorce, not defining marrigage. Such a reading reads into the text what is not there. And the Genesis definition of marriage doesn't necessarily exclude same-sex couples either. (http://www.gaychristian101.com/did-jesus-define-marriage-as-only-between-a-man-and-a-woman.html)

  7. When Jesus spoke against porneia what sins do you think he was forbidding?

    porneia: "fornication, whoredom; met: idolatry" (Strong’s Greek), "a selling off (surrendering) of sexual purity" (HELPS Word-studies) (http://biblehub.com/greek/4202.htm)

    Porneia is translated sexual immorality, whoredom, fornication, and idolatry. It is derived from porné which means prostitute, so I would conclude that porneia has to do with the selling of sex. There is no evidence that porneia is inclusive of homosexuality.

  8. If some homosexual behavior is acceptable, how do you understand the sinful "exchange" Paul highlights in Romans 1?

    Romans 18-20 NIV: The wrath of God is being revealed from heaven against all the godlessness and wickedness of people, who suppress the truth by their wickedness, since what may be known about God is plain to them, because God has made it plain to them. For since the creation of the world God’s invisible qualities—his eternal power and divine nature—have been clearly seen, being understood from what has been made, so that people are without excuse.

    Romans 1:21-23 NIV: For although they knew God, they neither glorified him as God nor gave thanks to him, but their thinking became futile and their foolish hearts were darkened. Although they claimed to be wise, they became fools and exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images made to look like a mortal human being and birds and animals and reptiles.

    In verses 21-23, Paul says that they "exchanged the glory of the immortal God for images made to look like a mortal human being and birds and animals and reptiles" because they knew God and did not glorify Him. This is a direct reference to idol worship.

    Romans 1:25-25 NIV: Therefore God gave them over in the sinful desires of their hearts to sexual impurity for the degrading of their bodies with one another. They exchanged the truth about God for a lie, and worshiped and served created things rather than the Creator—who is forever praised. Amen.

    (transition "therefore") In verses 24-27, Paul says that the people "exchanged the truth about God for a lie" and as a result "worshiped and served created things rather than the Creator." This is also a direct reference to idol worship.

    Romans 1:26-27 NIV: Because of this, God gave them over to shameful lusts. Even their women exchanged natural sexual relations for unnatural ones. In the same way the men also abandoned natural relations with women and were inflamed with lust for one another. Men committed shameful acts with other men, and received in themselves the due penalty for their error.

    (transition "because of this") In verses 26-27, Paul mentions "shameful lusts" and "[abandoning] natural relations with women". Paul was making an argument against idolatry A common part of idol worship in Paul’s day was shrine temple sex -- men who were married to women would have extramarital sex with shrine temple prostitutes in order to please the fertility god. The sinful exchange in Romans 1 is not homosexuality. It is idolatry.

    Romans 1:26-27 is not a description of a person with an innate, immutable same-sex attraction who is pursuing a committed, loving relationship with a person of the same sex. We can easily tell the difference between committed love and lustful idolatry in regard to opposite-sex relationships. We only ask that you see the same difference in regard to same-sex relationships.

  9. Do you believe that passages like 1 Corinthians 6:9 and Revelation 21:8 teach that sexual immorality can keep you out of heaven?

    I believe that Jesus died for all sins, including sexual immorality.

  10. What sexual sins do you think they were referring to?

    Put simply, sex outside a committed, lifelong relationship (i.e. marriage): rape, adultery, prostitution, etc. And I believe that forgiveness is available for every one of these.

    1 Corinthians 6:9-10 uses two terms that, in recent translations, are rendered to refer to some form of homosexual behaviour. While Paul most likely was referring to certain forms of homosexual behaviour, malakoi and arsenokoitai do not refer to homosexuality as an orientation or inside a committed relationship. Malakoi was not rendered as "homosexual" until the translation of the Amplified Bible in 1958. This is a relatively new change in translation.

    When anti-gay conservative Calvinists like Phil Johnson support the gay Christian view, that the cultural, historical, religious context of 1 Corinthians 6 was temple prostitution, that is an important admission. Honest students of the Bible should pay attention to Phil Johnson on this issue and consider the possibility that malakoi and arsenokoitai described shrine prostitutes who served pagan gods in the idol temples of Corinth. That form of idolatry was a problem for Jews in the Old Testament and Christians in the New Testament.

    ~~ http://www.gaychristian101.com/Malakoi.html ~~

  11. As you think about the long history of the church and the near universal disapproval of same-sex sexual activity, what do you think you understand about the Bible that Augustine, Aquinas, Calvin, and Luther failed to grasp?

    I think most people look at passages of scripture that do not directly affect them and simply take the text at it appears without looking at translation and context to derive the author’s intended meaning. I also understand sexual orientation in a way that these people likely did not -- and in a way that Paul did not. Paul referred to the behavior that he witnessed in his culture and society and rightly saw that it was wrong. However, homosexual behavior comes in different forms. Paul condemned lustful idolatry, not committed love between persons of the same sex.

    As noted above, the translation of malakoi has only recently been rendered as "homosexual." The interpretation and application of Scripture has changed over time. Examples include the view that Earth stands still, support for slavery, banning interracial marriage, and holding men as superior to women. These positions are now rightly viewed as incorrect, and I believe the position that committed, loving same-sex relationships are somehow sinful is also incorrect.

    Do you agree that interpretation of Scripture has changed over?

  12. What arguments would you use to explain to Christians in Africa, Asia, and South America that their understanding of homosexuality is biblically incorrect and your new understanding of homosexuality is not culturally conditioned?

    My understanding of homosexuality is not culturally conditioned. It is based on my personal experience. The idea that my feelings were somehow inherently wrong is what was culturally conditioned, and I’ve come to learn that I what I was taught was harmful to me. As for the first half your question, I would use the same arguments that I use here to convince Christians in the United States that homosexuality is not wrong.

    What arguments would you use to explain that your understanding of homosexuality is not culturally conditioned?

  13. Do you think Hillary Clinton and Barack Obama were motivated by personal animus and bigotry when they, for almost all of their lives, defined marriage as a covenant relationship between one man and one woman?

    I don't assume that anyone who disagrees with me is "motivated by personal animus and bigotry" when they disagree with my on this subject. That simply isn’t an assumption I make. And I have never called anyone a bigot. That word is not in my vocabulary, so to speak.

  14. Do you think children do best with a mother and a father?

    We have a lot of children who are in foster homes and who are waiting to be adopted. We allow single people to adopt children, and in any case, I believe that two parents are better than one.

  15. If not, what research would you point to in support of that conclusion?

    There is a lot of research that shows that children are not harmed by having same-sex parents.

    • http://www.eurekalert.org/pub_releases/2015-06/uoca-cdr061415.php

    • http://www.aamft.org/imis15/aamft/Content/Consumer_Updates/Same-sex_Parents_and_Their_Children.aspx

    • http://whatweknow.law.columbia.edu/topics/lgbt-equality/what-does-the-scholarly-research-say-about-the-wellbeing-of-children-with-gay-or-lesbian-parents/

    What research would you point to that says that same-sex couples are somehow inherently worse parents that opposite-sex parents?

  16. If yes, does the church or the state have any role to play in promoting or privileging the arrangement that puts children with a mom and a dad?

    N/A. I believe that same-sex couples should be allowed to adopt children, and I see no reason to keep children in a foster home or children’s home when there are couples available who are willing to love and care for them as their own. And adoption is undeniably a core part of God's plan.

  17. Does the end and purpose of marriage point to something more than an adult’s emotional and sexual fulfillment?

    Yes. Marriage is a reflection of Christ's love for the Church. And I see no reason that a Christ-centered relationship between a man and woman can reflect that while a Christ-centered relationship between persons of the same-sex cannot.

    But both emotional and sexual fulfillment are important in marriage.

  18. How would you define marriage?

    In the Christian sense, marriage is a committed, loving, monogamous, lifelong, Christ-centered relationship between two adults who seek to glorify God through their commitment to each other.

  19. Do you think close family members should be allowed to get married?

    No. And supporting same-sex marriage does not weaken that position.

  20. Should marriage be limited to only two people?

    Yes. And supporting same-sex marriage does not weaken that position.

  21. On what basis, if any, would you prevent consenting adults of any relation and of any number from getting married?

    Supporting same-sex marriage does not place the burden of disproving slippery slope arguments on me. A person in support of incest or polygamy would have to prevent their own arguments.

  22. Should there be an age requirement in this country for obtaining a marriage license?

    Yes.

  23. Does equality entail that anyone wanting to be married should be able to have any meaningful relationship defined as marriage?

    No.

  24. If not, why not?

    I do not believe children or closely related person should be get married. And my arguments against child marriage or incest marriage would probably be the same as yours, but I am not sliding down the slippery slope today. Marriage is a mutually consenting relationship/contract between adults.

  25. Should your brothers and sisters in Christ who disagree with homosexual practice be allowed to exercise their religious beliefs without fear of punishment, retribution, or coercion?

    Yes. But exercising your beliefs does not include discrimination in housing, employment, or public accommodations. Churches are free to discriminate as they always have been (e.g. Jewish rabbis are not required to perform marriages between Jews and non-Jews even after Loving v. Virginia).

    I believe that all employers, hospitals, and insurance companies, etc. hould be required to recognize all legal marriages for the purposes of benefits, medical visitation, and medical decision making, etc. I believe that all business that are open to the public should be required to serve their customers without discriminating based on sexual orientation or gender identity (and some jurisdictions have added one or both to their list of protected classes). I believe that discrimination against same-sex couple in housing would also be wrong.

  26. Will you speak up for your fellow Christians when their jobs, their accreditation, their reputation, and their freedoms are threatened because of this issue?

    It depends on the circumstance. If a state official loses their job or is moved to a new position for refusing to issue or sign legal marriage licenses, I will not speak out in their support. If a nurse, doctor, etc. refuses to care for a LGBT person or a person with same-sex or LGBT parents, I will not speak out in their support. If a hospital employee refuses to allow a patient’s spouse visitation or decision-making authority, I will not speak out in their support. If a pastor is punished by the state or federal government for refusing to marry any couple in his/her church, I will call that what it is: unconstitutional.

  27. Will you speak out against shaming and bullying of all kinds, whether against gays and lesbians or against Evangelicals and Catholics?

    Yes.

  28. Since the evangelical church has often failed to take unbiblical divorces and other sexual sins seriously, what steps will you take to ensure that gay marriages are healthy and accord with Scriptural principles?

    The same steps that should be taken to ensure that opposite-sex marriages "are healthy and accord with Scriptural principles." I'm not sure why it should be different.

  29. Should gay couples in open relationships be subject to church discipline?

    I am not not sure what the author means by "open relationships." If the author refers to discipline for a same-sex couple making their relationship public, absolutely not. If the author refers to discipline for a couple that is not strictly monogamous, I believe the same teachings applied to opposite-sex couples in regard to monogamy should be applied. If the latter is what the author meant by "open relationship," I should note that the question is out of place. LGBT persons should not be assumed to be automatically promiscuous just as straight persons aren’t.

  30. Is it a sin for LGBT persons to engage in sexual activity outside of marriage?

    Is is a sin for straight persons "to engage in sexual activity outside of marriage"? Same answer.

  31. What will open and affirming churches do to speak prophetically against divorce, fornication, pornography, and adultery wherever they are found?

    The same thing that non-affirming churches do expect for focusing on homosexuality being a sin as seems to be the pattern. The listed sins in this question are not inclusive of homosexuality.

  32. If "love wins," how would you define love?

    1 Corinthians 13 contains the best definition of love that I can find.

    My understanding of the #LoveWins campaign is that the love embodied by same-sex couples is not somehow lesser than the love embodied by opposite-sex couples.

    Would you define love in a way that excludes same-sex couples? If so, what basis in Scripture would you have to hold that persons of the same-sex cannot be in a loving relationship?

  33. What verses would you use to establish that definition?

    1 Corinthians 13.

  34. How should obedience to God’s commands shape our understanding of love?

    Obedience to God is certainly part of out individual expression of love to God. Scripture makes that clear, especially 1 John. However, my understanding of love certainly does not include constantly telling gay people are sinning just as I don’t constantly tell other people that they are sinning.

  35. Do you believe it is possible to love someone and disagree with important decisions they make?

    Yes. And based on the subtext of this question, I must note that being gay (or LGBT in general) is not a choice.

  36. If supporting gay marriage is a change for you, has anything else changed in your understanding of faith?

    Support of same-sex marriage is not a change for me, but I’ll answer the question. My faith is constantly improving. Over the past several years, I have learned the importance of service. I serve very faithfully in my church and in my campus ministry. And over the past two years, I have learned the importance of community - the importance of having people around me who care about me and the importance of caring about them.

  37. As an evangelical, how has your support for gay marriage helped you become more passionate about traditional evangelical distinctives like a focus on being born again, the substitutionary sacrifice of Christ on the cross, the total trustworthiness of the Bible, and the urgent need to evangelize the lost?

    My support of same-sex marriage doesn’t diminish my "focus on being born again, the substitutionary sacrifice of Christ on the cross, the total trustworthiness of the Bible, and the urgent need to evangelize the lost" in any way. I certainly believe in the trustworthiness of the Bible, but that also includes understanding the original intent of the authors. Being in a minority that is viewed negatively by many in the Church helps me to understand that Jesus is available for everyone. Jesus himself ministered primarily to the outcast minorities of his day.

  38. What open and affirming churches would you point to where people are being converted to orthodox Christianity, sinners are being warned of judgment and called to repentance, and missionaries are being sent out to plant churches among unreached peoples?

    I really doubt that those qualities are missing in all open and affirming churches. But not all churches minister to people via warnings of judgement. Love should come first. I know that open and affirming churches are trying their best to reach out to LGBT persons who have been rejected by other churches because their "warnings of judgement" displaced their love.

    Do you think that the highly negative impact more traditional/conservative churches have had on the LGBT community is "good fruit?" If yes, explain. If not, what you plan to do to correct it?

  39. Do you hope to be more committed to the church, more committed to Christ, and more committed to the Scriptures in the years ahead?

    Yes, as if my support of same-sex marriage would somehow change that. I always seek to deepen my understanding of Christ’s love for us.

  40. When Paul at the end of Romans 1 rebukes "those who practice such things" and those who "give approval to those who practice them," what sins do you think he has in mind?

    Romans 1:29-32 NIV: They have become filled with every kind of wickedness, evil, greed and depravity. They are full of envy, murder, strife, deceit and malice. They are gossips, slanderers, God-haters, insolent, arrogant and boastful; they invent ways of doing evil; they disobey their parents; they have no understanding, no fidelity, no love, no mercy. Although they know God’s righteous decree that those who do such things deserve death, they not only continue to do these very things but also approve of those who practice them.

    directly from the text: envy, murder, strife, deceit, malice, gossiping, slanderers, God-haters, insolent, arrogant, boasting, disobedience to parents, lack of understanding, lack of fidelity, lack of love, lack of mercy

    Romans 1:18-32 is discussions about the root and effects of idolatry. And committed, loving relationships between persons of the same sex are not condemned. The only discussion of homosexual behavior in Romans 1, or actually in all of the Bible, is behavior in relation to idolatry, specifically shrine temple sex.
